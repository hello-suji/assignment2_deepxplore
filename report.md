## problem 2 essay

Adversarial attacks can be combined with differential testing, but their role should be limited. In our experiment, differential testing on two ResNet50 models trained on CIFAR-10 produced 20 disagreement-inducing inputs. This result already shows that model diversity can expose unstable prediction regions. Most disagreements appeared on ambiguous images. Objects that share visual features across classes, such as cat and dog or automobile and truck, were more likely to produce inconsistent predictions. These cases are likely close to decision boundaries, but each model places that boundary slightly differently. Differential testing captures this difference directly.

We were actually surprised by how easily disagreement appeared. Even with the same architecture and dataset, small differences in seed and learning rate were enough to produce inconsistent outputs. This was not expected. It suggests that model diversity itself plays a stronger role than we initially assumed.

Adversarial methods such as FGSM and PGD could make this process more targeted. FGSM applies a single gradient-based perturbation. PGD repeats the process under a constraint. Both methods push inputs toward regions where the model is less stable. If those inputs are evaluated across multiple models, disagreement is likely to increase more quickly than with natural inputs alone. The neuron coverage result was less useful than expected. Both models reached 1.0 coverage very quickly. This does not indicate thorough testing. It shows that the metric is too coarse in this setting. The lecture material also notes that basic neuron coverage saturates easily and does not correlate well with fault detection. Once saturation happens, the metric stops guiding the search. A combined method should therefore separate objectives. Disagreement should remain the primary signal. Adversarial loss can push inputs toward unstable regions. Coverage can be used only to avoid revisiting identical activation patterns.

There are also practical limits. Adversarial inputs are often hard to interpret. A small perturbation may cause disagreement without producing a meaningful or realistic test case. This matters in our setting, where the goal is not only to find differences but also to analyze them. Overall, adversarial attacks can extend differential testing, but they are most useful after basic disagreement search becomes less productive. Our experiment shows that even simple model variation already reveals a significant number of unstable cases.
